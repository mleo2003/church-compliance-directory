Verified Alaska filing points

1) Alaska nonprofit biennial report cycle

Alaska nonprofit corporations are registered and maintained through the Alaska Division of Corporations, Business and Professional Licensing (CBPL), part of the Department of Commerce, Community, and Economic Development. Unlike many states with annual reporting, Alaska requires a biennial report from nonprofit corporations, generally due on January 2 of even-numbered years. The report must include current officer, director, and registered agent information.

Failure to file can result in the entity being placed in "not good standing" status and eventually administratively dissolved. Alaska's biennial report can be filed online through the CBPL business portal.

Churches operating in Alaska as nonprofit corporations should also maintain a valid registered agent in the state. If your church was formed under a national denomination's umbrella entity structure, verify how the state entity is registered.

Operational takeaway: The biennial cycle makes this easy to forget — set a two-year recurring reminder and check the CBPL portal for your entity's next due date.

2) Alaska payroll tax obligations — unique state context

Alaska is one of only a few states with no state income tax, which means there is no state income tax withholding obligation for Alaska employers. This significantly simplifies payroll compliance compared to most states.

However, Alaska does have unemployment insurance (UI) obligations for covered employers. UI is administered by the Alaska Department of Labor and Workforce Development. Employers file quarterly reports and pay UI contributions using the Employer Quarterly Contribution Report (TQ form). Alaska also has an Alaska Employment Security Tax component. Churches may qualify as reimbursable employers for UI (paying dollar-for-dollar for claims rather than quarterly contributions) — confirm with the Alaska DOLWD.

Alaska also has a unique community dividend / SUI structure worth confirming with a payroll advisor if your church has employees.

Operational takeaway: No state income tax simplifies withholding, but UI obligations still apply. Confirm your UI account status and filing schedule with the Alaska Department of Labor.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. "We'll remember later" for biennial filing windows (two years goes faster than expected)
  3. Payroll and governance tasks treated as separate systems
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
