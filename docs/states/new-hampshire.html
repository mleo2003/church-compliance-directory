Church Compliance Deadlines in New Hampshire (2026)

This is a directory of publicly available official resources — not legal or tax advice. We link to official state and federal agency pages so church admins can find the right starting point. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed attorney or CPA.

If you run admin for a New Hampshire church, the pain is usually not "what to do" — it's remembering when to do it and who owns it. This draft guide is designed to make that easier.

Who this applies to

  • Church admins, executive pastors, finance teams, and board secretaries
  • Churches with employees and recurring payroll obligations

Verified New Hampshire filing points

1) New Hampshire nonprofit registration/reporting cycle

New Hampshire nonprofits incorporated as nonprofit corporations must file an annual report with the New Hampshire Secretary of State. The annual report is due on April 1 of each year. The report updates the organization's registered agent, principal office address, and officer/director information. Filings are managed through the Secretary of State's QuickStart online portal.

New Hampshire also requires charities that solicit contributions from the public to register with and report annually to the New Hampshire Department of Justice, Charitable Trusts Unit. Even churches that are generally exempt from registration requirements under RSA 7:19 should confirm their specific exemption status, as the exemption landscape for churches in NH is defined by statute and may depend on how contributions are solicited.

Maintaining a current registered agent with a New Hampshire address is also required to keep the corporation in good standing.

Operational takeaway: New Hampshire churches likely face two separate annual filing obligations — one with the Secretary of State and potentially one with the AG's Charitable Trusts Unit. Know which agencies apply to your entity.

2) New Hampshire payroll tax obligations

New Hampshire has no state income tax on wages, so there is no state income tax withholding obligation for employers. However, New Hampshire does have unemployment insurance requirements:

  • Unemployment insurance: The New Hampshire Department of Employment Security administers UI. Employers, including nonprofit churches with employees, are generally subject to unemployment insurance contributions and must file quarterly wage reports and contribution returns. Nonprofits may qualify for reimbursing employer status as an alternative to the contribution method.

New Hampshire also imposes an Interest and Dividends Tax (scheduled to be phased out) and a Business Profits Tax / Business Enterprise Tax, but these typically do not apply to churches operating as exempt nonprofits. Confirm your entity's specific status with a tax advisor.

Operational takeaway: no income tax withholding does not mean zero payroll compliance — quarterly UI filings with the Department of Employment Security are still required for churches with employees.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

New Hampshire churches holding 501(c)(3) status should confirm whether their specific structure requires a Form 990, 990-EZ, or 990-N (e-Postcard), or whether their entity qualifies for the church exception. The absence of a state income tax does not eliminate federal filing obligations.

Operational takeaway: don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (Secretary of State + AG Charitable Trusts + UI + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify registered agent and officer information is current
  • Confirm UI quarterly filing workflow is on track with Department of Employment Security
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. The AG Charitable Trusts annual report is forgotten because it's separate from the Secretary of State filing
  3. Churches assume "no income tax = no payroll filings" and miss unemployment insurance obligations
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
