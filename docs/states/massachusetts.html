Verified Massachusetts filing points

1) Massachusetts nonprofit registration/reporting cycle

Massachusetts nonprofit corporations file an Annual Report with the Massachusetts Secretary of the Commonwealth. The report is due annually, with the deadline tied to the organization's fiscal year end.

Additionally, Massachusetts has a separate public charity filing requirement administered by the Attorney General's Non-Profit Organizations/Public Charities Division. Most public charities registered in Massachusetts must file an Annual PC (Public Charities) Filing each year. However, religious organizations are generally exempt from the public charity registration and annual filing requirements under Massachusetts law (M.G.L. c. 12, § 8F).

Key considerations: - The Secretary of the Commonwealth annual report maintains corporate good standing — this is distinct from the AG public charity filing - Annual reports are filed through the Massachusetts Corporations Division online system (Corporations Online) - Religious organizations should verify whether their exemption from AG registration applies — affiliated entities (schools, foundations) typically do not share the exemption - Massachusetts requires proper maintenance of corporate records, including board meeting minutes and updated officer/director information

Operational takeaway: Massachusetts has two reporting tracks (Secretary of the Commonwealth and Attorney General) that apply differently to churches. Know which your organization is subject to and set deadlines accordingly.

  • Massachusetts Secretary of the Commonwealth — Corporations Division: https://corp.sec.state.ma.us/CorpWeb/CorpSearch/CorpSearch.aspx
  • Massachusetts Attorney General — Non-Profit Organizations/Public Charities: https://www.mass.gov/orgs/non-profit-organizations-public-charities-division

2) Massachusetts payroll tax obligations

Massachusetts churches with employees must comply with Massachusetts Department of Revenue withholding requirements, as well as the Massachusetts Paid Family and Medical Leave (PFML) program.

Key agency and forms: - Massachusetts Department of Revenue administers state income tax withholding - Employers file Massachusetts Employer Withholding returns — filing frequency (monthly, quarterly, or annual) is assigned based on withholding volume - Annual W-2 reconciliation is required (Form M-3) - Massachusetts Department of Family and Medical Leave administers PFML — employers are generally required to withhold PFML contributions and submit employer contributions unless a private plan exemption is approved - Department of Unemployment Assistance administers UI contributions — religious organizations have specific exemption options

Key considerations: - Massachusetts PFML is a significant compliance requirement that many out-of-state payroll providers underestimate — religious organizations are generally subject to PFML unless they have an approved exemption - UI: Massachusetts religious organizations may qualify for an election to reimburse benefits instead of paying quarterly contributions

Operational takeaway: Massachusetts has multiple employer compliance layers — income tax withholding, PFML, and UI — all with separate filing requirements. Churches with Massachusetts employees should use a payroll provider experienced with Massachusetts requirements.

  • Massachusetts Department of Revenue — Withholding Tax: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/withholding-taxes
  • Massachusetts Department of Family and Medical Leave — PFML: https://www.mass.gov/orgs/department-of-family-and-medical-leave
  • Massachusetts Department of Unemployment Assistance: https://www.mass.gov/orgs/department-of-unemployment-assistance

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Specifically: - Churches (as defined by the IRS) are generally not required to file Form 990, 990-EZ, or 990-N - However, church-affiliated organizations (separate nonprofits, foundations, school ministries) may be required to file depending on their structure and gross receipts - Filing a 990-N (e-Postcard) voluntarily is an option some churches use to maintain a public record of activity

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

  • IRS — Filing Requirements for Churches and Religious Organizations: https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/churches-religious-organizations/filing-requirements-for-churches-and-religious-organizations

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track (including PFML contributions)
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. Massachusetts PFML obligations missed entirely — especially by churches using out-of-state payroll providers
  3. Payroll and governance tasks treated as separate systems
  4. Confusion between Secretary of Commonwealth and AG filing requirements
