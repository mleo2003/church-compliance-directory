If you run admin for a Colorado church, the pain is usually not "what to do" — it's remembering when to do it and who owns it. This draft guide is designed to make that easier.

Verified Colorado filing points

1) Colorado nonprofit periodic report cycle

Colorado nonprofit corporations are registered and maintained through the Colorado Secretary of State. Colorado requires nonprofits to file a Periodic Report annually, due during the anniversary month of the entity's formation — similar to Arizona, there is no single statewide deadline; each organization's due date depends on when it was incorporated.

Colorado has moved entirely to online filing through the Secretary of State's business filing portal (Colorado SOS Business Center). The periodic report must include current registered agent information and principal office address. Failure to file can result in delinquent status and eventual administrative dissolution.

Operational takeaway: Log in to the Colorado SOS Business Center, check your entity's next periodic report due date, and set a recurring annual reminder 60 days before that anniversary month.

2) Colorado payroll tax obligations

Colorado employers have several payroll tax compliance obligations:

State income tax withholding is administered by the Colorado Department of Revenue (DOR). Key forms: - DR 1094: Colorado W-2 Wage Withholding Tax Return (replaces older quarterly forms) — filed quarterly or on your assigned deposit schedule - DR 1107: Colorado 1099 Income Withholding Tax Return (if applicable) - Annual reconciliation with W-2 filing is required

Deposit frequency (annual, quarterly, monthly, or weekly) is based on annual withholding amounts. Colorado employers can file and pay through Revenue Online.

Unemployment Insurance (UI) is administered by the Colorado Department of Labor and Employment (CDLE). Employers file quarterly wage reports (UITR-1) and pay UI contributions. Nonprofit organizations may elect reimbursable employer status — confirm with CDLE.

Colorado FAMLI (Family and Medical Leave Insurance) — Colorado launched its paid family and medical leave program. Employers with one or more employees must remit quarterly FAMLI contributions split between employer and employee. Small employers (fewer than 10 employees) have a reduced employer share. This is a newer obligation churches should verify they are enrolled in and tracking.

Operational takeaway: Colorado's payroll stack is more complex than many states due to FAMLI. Confirm you are registered and current for withholding, UI, and FAMLI.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track (including FAMLI)
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. Anniversary-month periodic report missed because there's no fixed statewide deadline
  3. FAMLI contributions overlooked or misclassified — newer obligation still catching organizations off guard
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
