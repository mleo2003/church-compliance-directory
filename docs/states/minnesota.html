This is a directory of publicly available official resources — not legal or tax advice. We link to official state and federal agency pages so church admins can find the right starting point. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed attorney or CPA.

If you run admin for a Minnesota church, the pain is usually not "what to do" — it's remembering when to do it and who owns it. This draft guide is designed to make that easier.

Verified Minnesota filing points

1) Minnesota nonprofit registration/reporting cycle

Minnesota nonprofit corporations file an Annual Renewal with the Minnesota Secretary of State. The annual renewal is due by December 31 each year, regardless of fiscal year end.

Key considerations: - Minnesota's annual renewal is filed through the Minnesota Secretary of State's online system (Minnesota Business Filing System) - The renewal updates registered agent and principal office information - Failure to file by December 31 results in the organization being placed in "inactive" status, which can lead to dissolution - Minnesota has a separate charitable solicitation registration requirement administered by the Minnesota Attorney General's Office — religious organizations are generally exempt from this requirement under Minnesota Statutes § 309.515, subdivision 1, but affiliated organizations (schools, foundations) may not be; verify before assuming exemption

Operational takeaway: December 31 is a hard annual deadline in Minnesota. Unlike many states where the deadline varies by fiscal year, Minnesota's fixed year-end deadline applies to all nonprofits — which means it can get lost in holiday season. Build reminders starting in October.

  • Minnesota Secretary of State — Business Filings: https://www.sos.state.mn.us/business-liens/
  • Minnesota Secretary of State — Annual Renewal: https://mblsportal.sos.state.mn.us/Business/BusinessSearch

2) Minnesota payroll tax obligations

Minnesota churches with employees must comply with Minnesota Department of Revenue withholding requirements. Minnesota income tax withholding applies to wages paid to employees for services performed in Minnesota.

Key agency and forms: - Minnesota Department of Revenue administers state income tax withholding - Employers file Minnesota Employer Withholding returns — filing frequency (quarterly, monthly, or semi-weekly) is based on withholding volume - Annual W-2 reconciliation is required (Form MW508 equivalent) and W-2 forms must be distributed to employees - Minnesota Unemployment Insurance (UI) is administered by the Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development (DEED) — religious organizations may have specific exemptions and election options

Key considerations: - Minnesota UI: churches and religious organizations may be exempt from paying regular UI contributions but can elect to become liable employers or can self-insure; this requires deliberate election, not just assumption of exemption - Minnesota also has a Paid Leave program (effective 2026) — verify your church's obligations under the new Minnesota Paid Leave law, as it applies to most Minnesota employers

Operational takeaway: Minnesota's new Paid Leave program (effective January 2026) adds a new employer compliance layer that many churches have not yet addressed. Confirm your obligations with a Minnesota-familiar payroll provider or CPA.

  • Minnesota Department of Revenue — Employer Withholding: https://www.revenue.state.mn.us/employer-withholding
  • Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development — Unemployment Insurance: https://uimn.org/employers/
  • Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development — Paid Leave: https://paidleave.mn.gov/employers/

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Specifically: - Churches (as defined by the IRS) are generally not required to file Form 990, 990-EZ, or 990-N - However, church-affiliated organizations (separate nonprofits, foundations, school ministries) may be required to file depending on their structure and gross receipts - Filing a 990-N (e-Postcard) voluntarily is an option some churches use to maintain a public record of activity

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

  • IRS — Filing Requirements for Churches and Religious Organizations: https://www.irs.gov/charities-non-profits/churches-religious-organizations/filing-requirements-for-churches-and-religious-organizations

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track (including MN Paid Leave)
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. Missing the December 31 annual renewal — it falls during the holiday season and gets overlooked
  3. Minnesota Paid Leave obligations not yet addressed by churches entering 2026
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
