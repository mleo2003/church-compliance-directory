Verified Montana filing points

1) Montana nonprofit registration/reporting cycle

Montana nonprofits — including churches incorporated as nonprofit corporations — are registered with the Montana Secretary of State and must file an annual report to remain in good standing. The annual report is due by April 15 each year for corporations. Reports are filed online through the Secretary of State's Business Services portal.

Churches that are not incorporated but hold title to property or employ staff may still wish to formalize their status. Montana also requires nonprofits to maintain a registered agent with a Montana street address on file.

If your church is organized as a nonprofit corporation and fails to file the annual report, Montana may administratively dissolve the entity, which creates significant legal and operational complications.

Operational takeaway: set a calendar reminder in late February to prepare and file your annual report well before the April 15 deadline.

2) Montana payroll tax obligations

Montana has a state income tax and requires employers — including churches with employees — to withhold and remit state income tax. The relevant agency is the Montana Department of Revenue.

  • Withholding filings: Churches with employees must register as an employer with the Department of Revenue and file withholding tax returns on a schedule determined by the volume of withholding (monthly or quarterly filers). Annual reconciliation is also required.
  • Unemployment insurance: Montana's Department of Labor and Industry, Unemployment Insurance Division administers unemployment insurance. Churches that employ workers may be subject to unemployment insurance contribution requirements unless they qualify for a reimbursing employer election available to some nonprofits. Quarterly wage reports and contributions are filed through the My.Montana.gov portal.

Operational takeaway: payroll filing should be treated as non-negotiable recurring compliance — both the Department of Revenue and Department of Labor and Industry have separate obligations.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Montana churches holding 501(c)(3) status should confirm whether their specific structure and activities require a Form 990, 990-EZ, or 990-N (e-Postcard), or whether their entity qualifies for the church exception. Montana itself does not impose a separate state income tax on nonprofits, but this does not affect federal filing decisions.

Operational takeaway: don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings (registered agent, officers, address)
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track with Department of Revenue schedule
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. "We'll remember later" for the April annual report deadline
  3. Payroll and governance tasks treated as separate systems
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
