Church Compliance Deadlines in South Dakota (2026)

This is a directory of publicly available official resources — not legal or tax advice. We link to official state and federal agency pages so church admins can find the right starting point. For advice specific to your situation, consult a licensed attorney or CPA.

South Dakota has one of the lightest state tax burdens in the country — no state income tax, no corporate income tax, and a relatively streamlined nonprofit reporting environment. For church administrators, that means fewer state-level filings than most states. But the absence of state income tax doesn't eliminate payroll obligations, and the Secretary of State still requires nonprofits to maintain their registered status through annual filings. Missing even a low-burden deadline can result in administrative dissolution that's expensive and embarrassing to reverse.

Who this applies to

  • South Dakota churches that operate as nonprofit entities
  • Church admins, executive pastors, finance teams, and board secretaries
  • Churches with employees and recurring payroll obligations

Verified South Dakota filing points

1) South Dakota nonprofit registration/reporting cycle

South Dakota requires nonprofit corporations to file an annual report with the Secretary of State. The annual report is due by August 1 each year. The filing can be completed through the SD Secretary of State's online portal and carries a modest filing fee. Failure to file results in administrative dissolution, which requires reinstatement and additional fees to resolve.

South Dakota does not have a separate charitable solicitation registration requirement for religious organizations or nonprofits in the traditional sense — there is no state-level charity registration statute comparable to many other states. However, maintaining the nonprofit corporation's annual report filing with the Secretary of State remains the primary ongoing compliance obligation for corporate status.

Churches incorporated in South Dakota should also review their registered agent information annually. If the registered agent or principal office address changes, an updated filing with the Secretary of State is required. This is a common oversight that can result in missed legal notices.

Operational takeaway: Mark August 1 on your compliance calendar as a hard annual deadline. File the annual report through the SD Secretary of State's online business services portal and retain confirmation of filing in your governance records.

2) South Dakota payroll tax obligations

South Dakota has no state income tax, which means there is no state income tax withholding requirement for employee wages. This significantly reduces the payroll tax filing burden compared to most other states — there are no state withholding returns to file, no state W-2 reconciliation, and no state income tax deposits.

However, South Dakota employers — including churches with paid staff — are required to participate in the state unemployment insurance (UI) system administered by the South Dakota Department of Labor and Regulation. Employers must register, file quarterly wage reports, and pay UI contributions unless specifically exempt. Religious organizations may qualify for an exemption from state unemployment tax contributions under certain conditions, but the exemption is not automatic — churches must review their status and, if applicable, elect reimbursing employer status rather than the standard contribution method.

Quarterly UI reports are due on the last day of the month following the close of each calendar quarter (April 30, July 31, October 31, January 31). Late filing results in penalties and interest.

Operational takeaway: Confirm with the SD Department of Labor and Regulation whether your church is subject to UI contributions and whether the reimbursing employer election is appropriate. Since there is no state income tax withholding, your payroll tax compliance focus in South Dakota is unemployment insurance only.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify any org info changes that require updated filings
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. "We'll remember later" for state filing windows
  3. Payroll and governance tasks treated as separate systems
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
