Verified Indiana filing points

1) Indiana nonprofit biennial report cycle

Indiana nonprofit corporations are registered through the Indiana Secretary of State, Business Services Division. Indiana requires nonprofits to file a Business Entity Report (biennial report) every two years, due by the end of the anniversary month of the entity's incorporation. Unlike annual-report states, Indiana's biennial cycle means the report is only due every other year — but this also means it's easier to lose track of.

Indiana offers online filing through INBiz (the Indiana Business Center). The biennial report must include current officer/director information, registered agent details, and principal office address. Failure to file can result in administrative dissolution.

Note: Indiana does not have a statewide charitable solicitation registration requirement, which reduces one layer of compliance compared to many states.

Operational takeaway: Because the cycle is two years, set a calendar reminder 90 days before your anniversary month in the filing year. Check INBiz to confirm your entity's next due year.

2) Indiana payroll tax obligations — including county-level withholding

Indiana's payroll compliance has a unique layer that many out-of-state administrators miss: county income tax.

State income tax withholding is administered by the Indiana Department of Revenue (DOR): - Form WH-1: Indiana Employer's Withholding Tax Return — filed monthly, quarterly, or annually depending on withholding volume - Form WH-3: Indiana Annual Withholding Tax Report — annual reconciliation due by January 31 with W-2s

County income tax withholding: Indiana has a county-level income tax that employers must withhold based on each employee's county of residence (or county of principal work location for non-residents). The county tax rate varies by county. Employers report and remit county withholding alongside state withholding using WH-1, but must apply the correct county rate for each employee. This requires tracking employee county of residence carefully.

Unemployment Insurance (UI) is administered by the Indiana Department of Workforce Development (DWD). Employers file quarterly wage reports (UI-3) and pay UI contributions. Indiana nonprofit employers may qualify for reimbursable employer status — confirm with DWD.

Operational takeaway: Indiana's county income tax is a meaningful compliance wrinkle. Confirm your payroll system is calculating the correct county rate for each employee and that county information is updated when employees move. Verify your UI account and filing schedule with DWD.

3) Federal church filing requirement nuance

IRS guidance states that tax-exempt organizations generally file annual returns, but churches and some church-affiliated organizations may be excepted.

Operational takeaway: Don't assume "all churches file 990" or "no churches file anything." Validate your exact entity/facts with your tax advisor.

Monthly church compliance checklist (practical)

  • Review upcoming 90-day deadlines (state + payroll + governance)
  • Confirm owner and status for each open item
  • Verify employee county of residence is current in payroll system
  • Confirm payroll reporting/deposit workflow is on track (WH-1, UI-3)
  • Capture board decisions/minutes and any policy updates
  • Verify volunteer screening/training due dates

Common failure patterns we keep seeing

  1. Compliance is tracked in scattered spreadsheets with no owner accountability
  2. Biennial report missed because two-year cycles are easier to forget
  3. County income tax withholding calculated at wrong rate because employee address updates are not pushed to payroll
  4. No monthly review ritual, so deadlines are discovered late
